## üî• MAJOR DISSONANCE 1: Elementary vs Grothendieck Topos Terminology

**Key Players**: Kevin Carlson, Alex Kreitzberg, John Baez

**The Tension**: What does "topos" even mean?

> **Kevin Carlson** (2025-11-19): "A category of presheaves *is* a category of sheaves (ie Grothendieck topos), which *is* an elementary topos, which *is* category with a subobject classifier. But all these implications are irreversible. Most category theorists saying 'topos' mean 'elementary topos', but for historical reasons and the fact that Grothendieck toposes are pretty great, it's far from unheard of to use 'topos' to mean 'Grothendieck topos.'"

> **Alex Kreitzberg** (2025-11-19): "I tried to reverse engineer how the terminology is used on the nlab, but I wouldn't be surprised if my usage, or understanding, are confused... I found this odd, because i[t was] up to the reader to establish from context whether it was a grothendieck topos or an elementary topos."

> **Fernando Yamauti** (2025-11-04): "'Topos' for me means always 'Grothendieck topos'"

## üî• MAJOR DISSONANCE 2: ZFC vs ETCS Foundations

**Key Players**: David Michael Roberts, Mike Shulman, Kevin Carlson, John Baez

**The Tension**: Do you need sets to define categories?

> **David Michael Roberts** (2025-09-17): "Thinking that one needs sets to define categories is a fallacy that people entrenched in set theory bring up when trying to belittle using eg ETCS as a foundation."

> **Mike Shulman** (2025-09-17): "You can't define first-order syntax formally without some kind of sets. But they don't have to be ZFC-sets, they could equally well be ETCS-sets."

> **David Michael Roberts** (2025-10-22): "In the 1960s you had two things happen: Lawvere invented ETCS, a set theory that suffices for 99.99% of non-set-theory mathematics... and the invention of forcing by Cohen, which showed set theorists that essentially the ZFC axioms of set theory severely underdetermine how sets behave."

> **John Baez** (2025-10-16): "You are blending category theory and traditional logic (untyped first-order logic and material set theory like ZFC) in a way that seems not to have been studied as much as it should be... Why is this? I think it's because people who care about 'syntactic categories' tend to shun untyped first-order logic and material set theory."

## üî• MAJOR DISSONANCE 3: Structural vs Material Set Theory

**Key Players**: Amar Hadzihasanovic, John Baez

**The Tension**: The nature of mathematical "matter"

> **Amar Hadzihasanovic** (2025-12-09): "While category theory is often associated with mathematical structuralism, I would say that the particular flavour of CT is less of a choice of structuralism over materialism, and more of a 'blurring of boundaries' between the two, a 'monistic' approach... Set-theoretic mathematics have a kind of built-in dualism where 'structure' is at the logical level, 'matter' is sets, and 'implementation' or 'interpretation' is a one-way channel from logic to sets; in CT, typically, 'structure' and 'matter' are really just the domain and codomain of a functor."

## üî• MAJOR DISSONANCE 6: Lawvere's "Radical" Foundational Stance

**Key Players**: Gabriel Goren Roig, Matt Earnshaw, David Michael Roberts

**The Tension**: Lawvere's dismissal of inconsistency concerns

> **Gabriel Goren Roig** (2025-10-18): "There are many statements here that seem very strong to me: 'The past 100 years' tradition of foundations as justification has not helped mathematics very much.' The usage of the word 'foundation' in the sense of justification is *reactionary*. The attention given to the possibility that mathematics is consistent has been 'excessive' for the past century."

> **Matt Earnshaw** (2025-10-23): "I don't think that by 'alleged', Lawvere meant to call the study of inconsistency as such into question. Rather, it is important that he says 'alleged inconsistency *of mathematics*'. I do think it consistent to cast doubt on such a thing, because inconsistency is a property of formal systems, and it does not appear that the *global* practice of mathematics is taking [on that property]."
